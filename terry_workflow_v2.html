Terry's Workflow

Post-Meeting Action Item Summary — component by component

01
Starting Point
What kicks Terry's workflow off?
This is the condition that tells AI it is time to run.
Terry's starting point
Terry has two options here.
Option A
Manual: The meeting ends and Terry types a phrase — like "run meeting summary" — into his AI tool to kick it off.
Option B
Automatic: Depending on his tool, Terry can configure the workflow to trigger automatically when a new transcript syncs from Notion — no manual step needed.
Worth knowing: Most people start with the manual option and add automation later. Both are valid. Starting simple is always the right call.
02
Inputs
What does Terry give AI to work with?
Inputs are the raw material AI reads to do the job — not summaries, the actual source.
Terry's inputs
  • ›The meeting transcript
  • ›Meeting name and date
  • ›Attendees and their roles
  • ›Open items carried over from last week
On the transcript: Terry does not paste this in manually. His recording tool syncs transcripts to Notion automatically after each session, and his AI tool is connected to Notion via integration. The transcript arrives without him doing anything. Most AI tools support connections like this through their integrations or settings — it is usually a one-time setup.
03
Process
What steps does AI follow?
This is what Terry tells his AI tool to do once it has the inputs.
Terry's instructions to his AI tool
What Terry tells AI
"When I run this workflow, read the most recent meeting transcript from Notion. Identify every decision made and every task mentioned. For each task, extract what needs to be done, who owns it, and when it is due. If a due date was not stated, flag it as TBD. Then draft a structured summary using the format defined in my output instructions."
This is the prompt that lives inside the tool — not something Terry types fresh each time. He writes it once when setting up the agent, project, or skill. After that, it runs the same way every time.
04
Output
What does the finished product look like?
This is where Terry tells AI what good looks like — format, length, structure, and constraints.
Terry's output instruction to his AI tool
What Terry tells AI
"Produce a short structured summary every week. Start with one sentence capturing the key decision or theme of the meeting. Follow with a numbered list of action items — each item should include the owner's name, a one-sentence task description, and the due date. Keep the whole thing under 200 words. Plain language only, no jargon."
Why this matters: Without a precise output definition, AI guesses what you want — and you spend time editing instead of reviewing. The more specific you are here, the more consistent your results are every single week.
05
Review
Who checks it before it goes anywhere?
The review step is what makes a workflow trustworthy. It is not optional.
Terry's review step
Terry reads the summary before sharing it with the team. He checks that owners are correct, due dates match what was actually said, and nothing important was missed. Nothing goes to the team without his eyes on it first.
Remember: AI produces the draft. Terry decides when it goes. That is Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) — and for anything going to other people, it is the right default.
